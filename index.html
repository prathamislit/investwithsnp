Methodology  ·  What both projects share

Build the number
before the narrative.

Most risk discussion in finance and diligence terminates in qualitative output — country ratings, analyst commentary, management bios. These outputs cannot be backtested, compared across deals, or audited after the fact.

Both projects begin from the same constraint: produce a single, bounded, sector-normalised score from inputs that are themselves messy, qualitative, or partial. The score is the artefact. Everything else is supporting evidence.

Bounded score · Sector-normalised · Audit trail preserved

Publish the
false positives.

Validation discipline is the differentiator. Goldstein publishes a 29.2% false positive rate and a 252-day per-region warm-up exclusion. CHC explicitly disclaims predictive ability and frames itself as a descriptive diagnostic.

Neither project claims more than the data supports. The bar for a published coefficient is statistical significance after warmup exclusion. The bar for a published score is documented methodology and a reproducible pipeline.

Out-of-sample validation · Documented limitations · Reproducible

Research Approach

The question most
models don't ask.

Most geopolitical risk tools produce qualitative outputs — country ratings, analyst commentary, narrative indexes. They are not designed to be integrated into a quantitative risk framework because they produce no number that can be tested.

This research asks a different question: does geopolitical event data predict variance increases in linked financial instruments? Not returns. Not direction. Variance. The distinction matters because it determines whether the signal is actionable or merely interesting.

The answer, across 12 global chokepoints and four years of data, is that it does — with statistically significant coefficients and a validated hit rate that holds out of sample.

Methodology document available on request · 2022–2026 · Penn State

Signal construction
and validation.

The Geopolitical Risk Premium Score (GRPS) is built from three components: event-based instability drawn from GDELT and ACLED, sector volatility premium measured against rolling benchmarks, and VIX z-score conditioning for macro fear regime.

Each region runs through a 252-day warm-up period before any score is issued. No regime label is published until the full validation window closes. The false positive rate — 29.2% — is included in every summary. It is not withheld.

The scoring engine is not included in the public repository. The data pipeline, fetchers, quality checks, and backtest framework are open and documented.

github.com/prathamislit/Project-Goldstein · GDELT BigQuery · ACLED API
Finding I.
Geopolitical events
move volatility, not returns.

Across all 12 regions tested, γ coefficients for the geopolitical-to-variance relationship range from 0.784 to 0.934. No statistically significant return-prediction relationship was found. The variance channel is the operative one.

Finding II.
The signal holds
out of sample.

323 threshold-crossing events validated across a 21-day forward window. Average hit rate for volatility exceeding the 75th percentile post-crossing: 64.4%. The signal does not decay upon publication — it measures a structural regime, not an inefficiency.

Finding III.
Chokepoint selection
determines proxy.

The ETF proxy must be chosen to isolate region-specific exposure. Early iterations using XLE for multiple regions produced mathematically identical signals. Each of the 12 final proxies was selected to maximise independence and specificity of the geopolitical channel.

Finding IV.
Regime classification
is sufficient.

The continuous 0–100 score adds less information than the three-regime classification (STABLE · ELEVATED · CRITICAL) for practical risk management. The regime label is the actionable output. The score is the audit trail.

Finding V.
VIX conditioning
matters.

Incorporating VIX z-score as a macro fear conditioning variable reduced false positives by approximately 8 percentage points versus the unconditioned model. Global fear regime modulates how much geopolitical event data translates into sector-specific variance pressure.

Finding VI.
Warm-up exclusion
is non-negotiable.

The first 252 trading days per region are excluded from all validation — no exceptions. Models evaluated before sufficient data accumulation produce inflated coefficients. Published results reflect only the post-warmup window.

The Problem  ·  Why Human Capital Fails Due Diligence

The question most
models don't ask.

Private equity due diligence is rigorous where data is structured — financials, legal, tax, customer concentration. It becomes qualitative and unverifiable precisely where the risk is highest: the people running the business.

Management consulting firms have documented this gap for decades. Two-thirds of post-merger integration failures are attributed to culture and workforce issues, yet most deal memos allocate fewer than two pages to human capital — typically a brief management bio section.

The core problem is not that buyers ignore workforce health. It is that no structured, scoreable, comparable instrument exists to assess it. This framework proposes one.

McKinsey · Korn Ferry · Deloitte Insights · Harvard Business Review

Why private
mid-market?

Large-cap public targets have Glassdoor data, 10-K disclosures, analyst coverage, and LinkedIn headcount signals. They are far from easy to assess — but the signals exist.

Private companies with $50M–$500M EBITDA — the core of the middle market — have almost none of this. No EDGAR filings. No Glassdoor depth. No public attrition data. PE sponsors acquiring these companies are pricing workforce risk with almost no structured input.

This is the segment where the CHC Platform concentrates. The data architecture is built specifically for the constraints of private, mid-market targets: it anchors on public-record signals and primary survey data collected during diligence, rather than requiring historical disclosures the target has never made.

Mid-market: $50M–$500M EBITDA · ~$400–600B annual US PE volume (Pitchbook 2022–2024)
Framework  ·  The Five-Component HCV Index

The Human Capital Volatility (HCV) Index is the quantitative core of the CHC Platform. It produces a single composite score — bounded 0 to 1 — from five independent sub-components, each sourced from a different data layer. The score is sector-normalised against published industry benchmarks to enable cross-deal comparability.

TDS
Tenure & Departure
Signal
Encodes workforce seniority distribution and departure velocity from primary survey responses, weighted by role tier and functional criticality.
Weight: 20%
BDR
Business Disruption
Risk
Integrates passive external signals — job posting velocity, WARN Act notices, OSHA citations — into a market-observable disruption indicator. WARN Act notice triggers a hard score floor.
Weight: 25%
SDS
Survey-Derived
Signal
Anonymised primary survey data from a stratified sample of target employees during diligence. Twelve validated questions across leadership trust, psychological safety, and intent-to-stay dimensions.
Weight: 25%
VGS
Velocity & Growth
Signal
Structural growth indicators — headcount trajectory, role expansion ratios, hiring pattern analysis — normalised by sector baseline and company stage.
Weight: 15%
MCS
Market Comparator
Signal
Sector peer benchmarking via employment litigation records (PACER), executive departure events, and public peer-group comparator data to contextualise absolute scores.
Weight: 15%

Output: the
CHC Report

The platform produces a Cultural Health Certificate — a PDF artefact suitable for insertion into an M&A data room alongside financial and legal due diligence reports.

The report presents the composite HCV score, each sub-component with its contributing signals, a four-tier risk classification (GREEN / YELLOW / ORANGE / RED), sector-relative percentile placement, and a narrative flag section summarising material risks for the deal team.

The sell-side deployment model means the target company commissions the CHC during deal prep — typically 60–90 days before marketing — and includes it in the Virtual Data Room. Investment banks advising the process become the primary distribution channel.

Report: WeasyPrint / Jinja2 · Delivery: authenticated link · Turnaround: 72h from survey close

What the score
does not do.

The HCV Index is explicitly a descriptive diagnostic, not a predictive model. It does not claim to forecast post-close turnover rates or EBITDA impact with precision.

The framework makes a narrower, more defensible claim: that a structured, multi-signal composite score provides better due diligence coverage than unstructured management interviews alone — and produces a comparable, archivable record of the assessment.

Component weights draw on published practitioner literature (Korn Ferry Human Capital M&A research, Deloitte Human Capital Trends, Mercer workforce risk indices) and are designed to be recalibrated as engagement data accumulates.

Not a financial instrument · Not investment advice · Score is informational input

OSHA enforcement records and PACER federal court employment litigation dockets. Both are free public data with structured query access. No proprietary data required.

Historical job posting velocity and pattern analysis via Apify web infrastructure. Available for any company that has ever posted publicly — the primary passive signal for private mid-market targets without Glassdoor depth.
